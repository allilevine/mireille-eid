One handed Tourniquet
Simpler really is better. This is especially true when a wounded soldier, far from a buddy or medic, needs to put a tourniquet on a severely injured arm or leg.
Three years ago a research and development effort among three U.S. Army Medical Research and Materiel Command organizations set out to create a tourniquet that a wounded soldier could use with one hand.

Losing valuable Training DaysTwo viruses plaguing military training centers will once again meet their match when the U.S. Army Medical Research and Materiel Command and Barr Pharmaceuticals remanufacture their vaccines.
Adenoviruses type 4 and 7 produce symptoms like the flu but can cause military recruits to lose valuable training days while they recuperate from symptoms such as sore throat, fever, cough, runny nose, headache and chest pain.
